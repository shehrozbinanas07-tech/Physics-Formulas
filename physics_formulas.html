01

Motion, Forces & Energy

What is SUVAT? SUVAT is a memory aid for the five variables in uniform-acceleration motion:
S = displacement  ·  U = initial velocity  ·  V = final velocity  ·  A = acceleration  ·  T = time
Each equation below uses four of these five. Pick the one that contains the three variables you already know plus the one you want to find.
1.1 · Motion
Speed / Velocity
v = st
v = velocity (m/s)
s = displacement (m)
t = time (s)
m/s
Acceleration
a = v − ut
a = acceleration (m/s²)
v = final velocity (m/s)
u = initial velocity (m/s)
t = time (s)
m/s²
Equation of Motion ① — find v  (missing s)
v = u + at
Use when displacement is not needed
u = initial vel. · a = accel. · t = time
Equation of Motion ② — find s  (missing v)
s = ut + ½at²
Use when final velocity is not needed
s = displacement · u = initial vel.
Equation of Motion ③ — no t needed  (missing t)
v² = u² + 2as
Use when time is not known or needed
Links velocity directly to displacement
1.2 · Mass, Weight & Density
Weight
W = mg
W = weight (N)
m = mass (kg)
g = gravitational field strength = 10 N/kg on Earth
N
Density
ρ = mV
ρ (rho) = density (kg/m³)
m = mass (kg)
V = volume (m³)
kg/m³
Gravitational Field Strength
g = Wm
g = 10 N/kg on Earth · varies on other planets
W = weight (N) · m = mass (kg)
N/kg
1.3 · Forces & Momentum
Newton's 2nd Law
F = ma
F = resultant force (N)
m = mass (kg)
a = acceleration (m/s²)
N
Momentum
p = mv
p = momentum (kg·m/s)
m = mass (kg)
v = velocity (m/s)
kg·m/s
Impulse = Change in Momentum
Ft = mv − mu
F = force (N) · t = time (s)
mv = final momentum
mu = initial momentum
N·s
Moment of a Force (Turning Effect)
M = F × d
M = moment / torque (N·m)
F = force applied (N)
d = perpendicular distance from pivot (m)
N·m
1.4 · Pressure
Pressure
P = FA
P = pressure (Pa)
F = force pushing on surface (N)
A = contact area (m²)
Pa (N/m²)
Pressure in a Fluid (due to depth)
P = ρgh
ρ = density of the fluid (kg/m³)
g = 10 N/kg
h = depth below the surface (m)
Pa
Boyle's Law — constant temperature
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Pressure × volume = constant
P = pressure · V = volume
Only valid when temperature does not change
1.5 · Work, Energy & Power
Work Done
W = F × s
W = work done (J)
F = force in the direction of motion (N)
s = displacement (m)
J
Kinetic Energy
KE = ½mv²
Energy an object has due to its motion
m = mass (kg) · v = speed (m/s)
J
Change in Kinetic Energy
ΔKE = ½m(v² − u²)
v = final speed · u = initial speed
Equals the work done by the resultant force
J
Gravitational Potential Energy
GPE = mgh
Energy stored due to height above ground
m = mass (kg) · g = 10 N/kg · h = height (m)
J
Power
P = Wt  or  Et
P = power (W) · W or E = energy (J) · t = time (s)
Power = rate of energy transfer
W
Power (for a moving object)
P = Fv
Useful shortcut when speed is given
F = driving force (N) · v = speed (m/s)
W
Efficiency
η = Useful Energy Output Total Energy Input  × 100%
Unitless — always expressed as a percentage
100% = perfectly efficient (no energy wasted)
Wasted Energy
Ewasted = Einput − Euseful
Total energy is always conserved overall
Wasted energy is usually released as heat or sound
J
02

Thermal Physics

⚠ Always convert °C to Kelvin before using any gas law equation.
T (K) = T (°C) + 273  ·  Absolute zero = 0 K = −273 °C
2.1 · Temperature & Gas Laws
Kelvin Conversion
T(K) = T(°C) + 273
Always use Kelvin (K) in gas law calculations
You cannot use Celsius directly in these equations
K
Boyle's Law — temperature constant
P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Pressure and volume are inversely proportional
P = pressure (Pa) · V = volume (m³)
Pressure Law — volume constant
P₁T₁ = P₂T₂
Pressure is proportional to temperature
T must be in Kelvin · volume stays constant
Charles's Law — pressure constant
V₁T₁ = V₂T₂
Volume is proportional to temperature
T must be in Kelvin · pressure stays constant
2.2 · Thermal Energy
Specific Heat Capacity
Q = mcΔT
Heat needed to change temperature (no change of state)
Q = heat energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
c = specific heat capacity (J/kg·°C)
ΔT = temperature change (°C or K)
J
Specific Latent Heat
Q = mL
Heat needed to change state (temperature stays constant)
Q = heat energy (J)
m = mass (kg)
L = specific latent heat (J/kg)
Use Lf for melting/freezing · Lv for boiling/condensing
J

Waves

3.1 · General Wave Properties
Wave Speed
v = fλ
v = wave speed (m/s)
f = frequency (Hz)
λ (lambda) = wavelength (m)
m/s
Frequency and Period
f = 1T
f = frequency (Hz) — number of waves per second
T = period (s) — time for one complete wave
Hz
3.2 · Reflection, Refraction & Lenses
Refractive Index
n = sin isin r
n = refractive index (no unit — just a number)
i = angle of incidence (measured from normal)
r = angle of refraction (measured from normal)
Snell's Law — at a boundary
n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
Used when light crosses between two different materials
n₁, n₂ = refractive indices of each medium
Critical Angle
sin c = 1n
c = critical angle (degrees)
n = refractive index of the denser medium
Total internal reflection happens when angle > c
Magnification
m = image heightobject height = vu
v = image distance from lens (m)
u = object distance from lens (m)
m > 1 → magnified · m < 1 → diminished
3.3 · Sound & Echoes
Speed of Sound — use wave speed formula
v = fλ
Speed of sound in air ≈ 340 m/s
Speed of light / all EM waves = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
m/s
Echo — distance to object
d = v × t2
d = distance to reflecting object (m)
v = speed of sound (m/s)
t = total time measured for echo (s)
Divide by 2 because the sound travels there and back
m

Electricity & Magnetism

4.1 · Basic Quantities
Electric Charge
Q = It
Q = charge (C)
I = current (A)
t = time (s)
C
Ohm's Law
V = IR
V = voltage / potential difference (V)
I = current (A)
R = resistance (Ω)
V
Resistance
R = VI
Rearrangement of Ohm's Law
Higher resistance → less current for the same voltage
Ω
4.2 · Series & Parallel Circuits
Series — Total Resistance
RT = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Resistances simply add up
Same current flows through every component
Ω
Parallel — Total Resistance
1RT = 1R₁ + 1R₂ + 1R₃
RT is always less than the smallest individual R
Each branch shares the same voltage
Ω
Series — Voltage Rule
Vtotal = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
Voltage splits across each component
All the individual voltages add up to the supply voltage
V
Parallel — Current Rule
Itotal = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
Current splits between branches
Branch currents add up to total current
A
Voltage Divider Rule (series only)
V₁V₂ = R₁R₂
Only applies to resistors in series
Larger resistance → larger share of the voltage
4.3 · Power & Energy in Circuits
Electrical Power ① — general
P = IV
P = power (W) · I = current (A) · V = voltage (V)
Use this form when both I and V are known
W
Electrical Power ② — voltage unknown
P = I²R
Use when voltage is not given
Derived from P = IV and V = IR
W
Electrical Power ③ — current unknown
P = V²R
Use when current is not given
Derived from P = IV and I = V ÷ R
W
Electrical Energy Transferred
E = Pt = IVt
E = energy transferred (J)
P = power (W) · t = time (s)
J
4.4 · Transformer
Voltage Ratio
VpVs = NpNs
Vp = primary (input) voltage
Vs = secondary (output) voltage
Np = turns on primary coil
Ns = turns on secondary coil
Ideal Transformer — power in = power out
Vp × Ip = Vs × Is
Assumes 100% efficiency (no energy loss)
Step-up voltage → step-down current, and vice versa
W
Transformer Efficiency
η = Vs × Is Vp × Ip  × 100%
Unitless — expressed as a percentage
Real transformers are slightly less than 100%
05

Nuclear Physics

⚠ Conservation rule for all nuclear equations:
The total mass number (A) must be equal on both sides of the arrow.
The total atomic number (Z) must also be equal on both sides.
5.1 · Nuclear Notation
Nuclide Symbol
AZ X
A = mass number = protons + neutrons
Z = atomic number = number of protons
X = chemical symbol of the element
Number of Neutrons
N = A − Z
N = number of neutrons
Neutrons = mass number minus atomic number
5.2 · Radioactive Decay
Alpha Decay (α)
AZX  →  A−4Z−2Y  +  42He
A decreases by 4 · Z decreases by 2
Emits a helium-4 nucleus (the alpha particle)
Least penetrating — stopped by a sheet of paper
Beta-Minus Decay (β⁻)
AZX  →  AZ+1Y  +  0−1e
A unchanged · Z increases by 1
A neutron turns into a proton + an electron
Stopped by a few mm of aluminium
Gamma Emission (γ)
AZX*  →  AZX  +  γ
A and Z both stay the same — only energy is released
High-energy electromagnetic radiation
Usually follows alpha or beta decay
Needs thick lead or concrete to stop
5.3 · Half-Life
Remaining Activity After n Half-Lives
N = N₀ × 12 n
N = remaining number of nuclei (or activity)
N₀ = original number of nuclei (or activity)
n = number of half-lives that have passed
Each half-life → activity halves exactly
Number of Half-Lives Elapsed
n = total time elapsed t½
t½ = the half-life (in s, minutes, years, etc.)
n = how many half-lives have passed
Plug n into the formula above to find remaining activity

Space Physics

Note: Space physics in 5054 is largely conceptual. Understand stellar life cycles, the Big Bang, and red-shift in words — the equations below are the quantitative parts examiners can ask you to calculate.
6.1 · Key Equations
Speed of All EM Waves in a Vacuum
c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
This is the same for all electromagnetic radiation in space
It is also the fastest possible speed in the universe
m/s
Red-Shift
z = Δλλ₀
z = red-shift value (no unit)
Δλ = how much the wavelength has shifted
λ₀ = the original wavelength when at rest
Larger z → the galaxy is moving away faster
Hubble's Law
v = H₀ × d
v = speed at which a galaxy is moving away
H₀ = Hubble constant
d = distance to the galaxy
Evidence that the universe is expanding
km/s
Orbital Speed
v = 2πrT
r = orbital radius (m)
T = orbital period — time for one full orbit (s)
2πr = circumference of the circular orbit
m/s
6.2 · Gravity (revisited from Topic 1)
Weight in a Gravitational Field
W = mg
g on Earth ≈ 10 N/kg · on the Moon ≈ 1.6 N/kg
g gets weaker the further you are from a planet
Mass stays the same everywhere — weight changes
N
